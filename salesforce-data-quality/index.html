Data quality & governance — case study

Salesforce Data Quality at JLL Technologies

Reporting is only as good as the records underneath it. On the CRM operations side at JLL Technologies, my job was to keep those records clean, accurate, and consistent — the data-quality groundwork that lets a team trust the numbers it reports on.

Fabian Nevarez · Operational CRM support · JLL Technologies

The problem

In a CRM the size of an enterprise sales org, records drift. Duplicates accumulate, fields get entered inconsistently, ownership and status fall out of date, and required attributes go missing. Individually each one is small; in aggregate they quietly corrupt every report, dashboard, and pipeline forecast built on top of them. The reporting looks authoritative while resting on data no one has verified.

What I did

  • Record cleanup. Worked through Salesforce accounts and contacts to correct inconsistent, incomplete, and duplicated entries — normalizing how key fields were populated so the same thing was recorded the same way every time.
  • Accuracy audits. Reviewed CRM records against expected standards to catch errors before they reached a report — the same "check it against the source" discipline that keeps a dashboard honest.
  • Governance standards. Followed the team's data governance conventions for how records should be structured and maintained, so the cleanup held up over time instead of degrading right back.
  • Operational CRM support. Handled day-to-day CRM operations that kept the system usable for the people relying on it downstream.

Why it matters

Discipline

Audit before report

Outcome

Reporting you can trust

Standard

Consistent, governed records

Clean records aren't a cosmetic nicety — they're the precondition for every decision made off the CRM. When the underlying data is accurate and consistent, pipeline numbers, forecasts, and activity reports mean what they say. That reliability is the whole point of data-quality work.

The transferable skill

This is the same instinct behind my other projects: reconcile against the source, standardize before you summarize, and never report a number you haven't checked. In a CRM data-quality or governance role, that instinct is the job — catch the bad record before it becomes a bad decision, and put a standard in place so it doesn't recur.

Scope note: this was operational, data-quality and support-level CRM work — not a Salesforce administrator or certified-admin role. What it built is a genuine feel for what makes enterprise CRM data trustworthy, and the day-to-day habits that keep it that way.
